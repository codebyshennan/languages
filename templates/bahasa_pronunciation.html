Pronunciation Reference

A complete overview of Bahasa Indonesia vowels, consonants, stress, and affixation. Click on any row to hear the example word via text-to-speech.

Good news: Indonesian is one of the most phonetically consistent languages — spelling and pronunciation align closely. There are no tones, no grammatical gender, and verbs do not conjugate for tense. The main challenges are the two e sounds and the nasal digraphs.

1. Vowels

Indonesian has 6 vowel sounds. The trickiest is e, which has two distinct pronunciations — taling (clear "eh") and pepet (schwa "uh") — both written identically without diacritics. Context and dictionary lookup distinguishes them.

Vowel Approx. English Notes Example
a "father" Open, never "ay" — consistent in all positions apa
e (taling) "bed" Clear mid-front vowel — enak (tasty), sate, beli enak
e (pepet) "about" (schwa) Reduced central vowel — common in prefixes (me-, be-, ter-, ke-) and many roots benar
i "bee" High-front, pure — never lax as in English "bit" ini
o "boat" (pure) Mid-back rounded — no "oh-w" glide. Can open toward "aw" word-finally. obat
u "boot" (pure) High-back rounded — consistent; no "oo-w" glide untuk

2. Key Consonants & Digraphs

Most consonants match English. These are the exceptions and digraphs that learners must learn explicitly.

Spelling Sound Notes Example
c "ch" Always "ch" as in church — never "k" or "s" coba
g "g" Always hard g as in go — never soft as in English gem guna
h "h" / weakened Clear at start of word and between vowels; often weakened or silent at word end hari
j "dj" Like "judge" — a voiced affricate, not a French "j" jalan
kh "kh" Arabic loanword Fricative — like Scottish loch. Often simplified to "k" in colloquial speech. khusus
ng "ng" Like "sing" — can begin a syllable or word, unlike English dengan
ngg "ng" + hard g The nasal ng followed by a hard g — distinct from single ng tunggu
ny "ny" Like "canyon" or Spanish ñ — palatal nasal, can begin a word nyaman
r tapped / trilled Tongue tap or light trill — never the English "r". Pronounced clearly in all positions including word-final. rasa
sy "sh" Arabic loanword Like "show" — syarat (requirement), syukur (gratitude) syarat
v "f" or "v" In loanwords — many speakers use "f"; some use "v". Interchangeable in practice. visi
-k (final) glottal stop Word-final k is typically an unreleased glottal stop, not a full /k/ burst tidak

3. Stress

Indonesian stress is generally predictable but lighter and less prominent than in English.

Default rule: Stress falls on the penultimate (second-to-last) syllable.
Exception: If the penultimate syllable contains a pepet e (schwa), stress shifts to the final syllable instead.
Word Breakdown Rule
makan MA-kan Penultimate — standard rule
bicara bi-CA-ra Penultimate — standard rule
benar be-NAR Pepet e in penultimate → stress shifts to final
teman TE-man Penultimate e here is taling (clear "eh") → stays penultimate
sekolah se-KO-lah Pepet e in first syllable; penultimate is ko → standard penultimate

4. Prefixes & Nasal Assimilation

The active verb prefix me- undergoes nasal assimilation — its final consonant changes to match the first consonant of the root word. This is one of the most important pronunciation patterns in Indonesian.

me- assimilation rules: The nasal consonant added matches the place of articulation of the root's first consonant. Roots beginning with voiced stops (b, d, g, j) keep their consonant; roots beginning with voiceless stops (p, t, k, s) drop the initial consonant.
me- + b/f/v → mem-
Bilabial nasal — root consonant kept
membaca membeli membuat
me- + p → mem-, p drops
Bilabial nasal — voiceless p is dropped
memukul (← pukul) memakai (← pakai)
me- + d/j → men-
Alveolar nasal — root consonant kept
mendengar menjual
me- + t/c → men-, t drops
Alveolar nasal — voiceless t dropped; c treated as voiced
menulis (← tulis) mencari (← cari)
me- + g → meng-
Velar nasal — root consonant kept
menggunakan mengambil
me- + k → meng-, k drops
Velar nasal — voiceless k is dropped
mengirimi (← kirim) mengenal (← kenal)
me- + s → meny-, s drops
Palatal nasal — voiceless s is replaced by ny
menyukai (← suka) menyebut (← sebut)
me- + vowel → meng-
Velar nasal added before vowel-initial roots
mengambil mengingat mengucap

5. Colloquial Reductions

Everyday spoken Indonesian (especially in Jakarta) differs significantly from formal written Indonesian. These reductions are extremely common and you will hear them constantly.

Formal Colloquial Meaning
tidak nggak / gak / enggak no / not
sudah udah / dah already / done
belum blum not yet
saya aku / gue (Jakarta) I / me (formal → informal → Betawi slang)
kamu lo / lu (Jakarta) you (formal → Betawi slang)
bagaimana gimana how / what about
yang yang / yg that / which / who (written shorthand common in SMS/chat)
mengapa kenapa / knp why
