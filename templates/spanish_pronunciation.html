Pronunciation Reference

A complete overview of Spanish vowels, stress rules, and consonants. Click on any row to hear the example word via text-to-speech.

1. The 5 Vowels

Key point: Spanish vowels are pure and short — each one has a single, consistent sound. Unlike English, the vowels never diphthongize (e.g. Spanish o is always "oh", never "oh-w").
Vowel Approx. English Notes Example
a "father" Open, never "ay" casa
e "bed" Mid-front, never "ee" mesa
i "bee" High-front, always pure libro
o "boat" (pure) No "oh-w" glide como
u "boot" (pure) Rounded, no "oo-w" glide. Silent after q and in gue/gui. luna

2. Stress & Accent Marks

Spanish stress follows predictable rules. A written accent (tilde: á é í ó ú) always overrides them. The tilde never changes vowel quality — only stress placement.

Rule 1 — Penultimate stress
Words ending in a vowel, n, or s
ca-sa co-men li-bros ha-blan
Rule 2 — Final stress
Words ending in any other consonant
ciu-dad ha-blar fe-liz pa-red
Rule 3 — Tilde overrides
Written accent marks any exception
fá-cil ha-bló mú-si-ca ca-fé
Word Breakdown Rule applied
casa CA-sa Ends in vowel → penultimate
ciudad ciu-DAD Ends in -d → final
fácil FÁ-cil Ends in -l (→ final rule), tilde overrides to penultimate
música MÚ-si-ca Ends in vowel (→ penultimate), tilde overrides to antepenultimate
habló ha-BLÓ Ends in vowel (→ penultimate), tilde overrides to final

3. Key Consonants

Most Spanish consonants match their English equivalents. These are the ones that trip up learners.

Spelling Sound Notes Example
b / v "b" Both letters are identical in speech — no V-sound in Spanish vino / bien
c (+ e/i) "s" / "th" LatAm "s"   Spain "th" as in think cero
c (+ a/o/u) "k" Hard k — always casa
g (+ e/i) "kh" Harsh fricative like Scottish loch or German Bach gente
g (+ a/o/u) "g" Hard g — like "go" gato
gu (+ e/i) "g" The u is silent — spelling rule to keep hard g before e/i guerra
gü (+ e/i) "gw" Diaeresis (ü) means the u is pronounced pingüino
h silent Always silent in Spanish (except in borrowed words) hola
j "kh" Same harsh fricative as g + e/i; stronger in Spain jugar
ll "y" / "zh" LatAm like "y" in yes   Rioplatense "zh" like measure llama
ñ "ny" Like "canyon" or Spanish ñ — palatal nasal año
qu (+ e/i) "k" The u is always silent; spelling rule to keep hard k queso
r (initial) rolled "rr" At start of word or after n/l/s — always strongly trilled rosa
r (medial) single tap Between vowels — single tongue tap, like "tt" in American butter pero
rr rolled Strongly trilled — contrasts with single r; pero (but) vs perro (dog) perro
x "ks" / "s" / "kh" Usually "ks"; "s" in some prefixes (ex-); "kh" in place names (México, Oaxaca) taxi
y "y" / "zh" As consonant: same as ll — regional variation. As vowel (e.g. y = and): sounds like "ee" yo
z "s" / "th" LatAm "s"   Spain "th" as in think zapato

4. Regional Variation

Spanish is spoken across 20+ countries. These are the most significant pronunciation differences you'll encounter.

Seseo vs Distinción: In all of Latin America and the Canary Islands, c (before e/i) and z are pronounced as /s/. In most of Spain, they are pronounced as /θ/ ("th" as in think). Neither is "more correct" — both are standard.
Feature Spain Latin America Example
c (+ e/i) and z "th" (think) "s" zapato
ll and y (consonant) "y" (most regions) "y"; "zh" in Río de la Plata (Argentina, Uruguay) pollo
s (word-final) clear /s/ May weaken to /h/ or drop (Caribbean, coastal LatAm) estos
vosotros (2nd-pl informal) Common — habláis, coméis Not used — ustedes used instead habláis
d (intervocalic) Strongly fricative /ð/ Varies — can be weakened or dropped (e.g. cansado → "cansao") ciudad

5. Diphthongs & Hiatus

When two vowels appear together, they either form a diphthong (one syllable) or a hiatus (two syllables). The weak vowels are i and u; the strong vowels are a, e, o.

Diphthong (one syllable): weak + strong, or weak + weak — ai, ei, oi, au, eu, ia, ie, io, ua, ue, uo, ui, iu
Hiatus (two syllables): strong + strong, or weak with tilde — ae, ao, ea, oa, eo or ío, úa
Type Vowels Syllables Example
Diphthong ie tie-ne (2) tiene
Diphthong ue pue-de (2) puede
Diphthong au au-to (2) auto
Diphthong ai / ay ai-re (2) aire
Hiatus (strong+strong) ea re-al (2) real
Hiatus (tilde breaks diphthong) ío rí-o (2) río
Hiatus (tilde breaks diphthong) úa con-ti-nú-a (4) continúa
